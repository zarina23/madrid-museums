Museums of Madrid

When it comes to major cultural attractions, this European capital is especially blessed. The museums in Madrid are great places to experience the city’s love for history, art, and culture. Not only can art lovers find an extensive collection of paintings by renowned artists like Goya, Dali, and Velázquez, there are plenty of unique museums tailored for visitors of all ages.

Culture and art for all budgets - that is what Madrid offers throughout the year. From the big art galleries to other museums of reference, Madrid gives us the opportunity to admire its art free of charge on certain days and times. Keep reading and find out!

Art Museums

Many European capitals are known for their impressive scale of museums and galleries, but when it comes to Madrid, there is something particularly unique. Three of its most illustrious museums are neighbors along the same street, with many other well-known cultural centers not far away. The Paseo del Arte (or Art Walk, which takes place between the three top art museums) makes it especially convenient for visitors to take in such celebrated masterpieces and works of art. Who doesn’t love easy access to major cultural attractions? While there are numerous art museums and galleries in Madrid, we will focus on the three most renowned ones.

Prado Museum

The Prado is a collection of Spanish and international masterpieces—and a source of national pride. Architect Juan de Villanueva designed numerous neoclassical works around the city, but none quite as celebrated as this one. Originally the National History Cabinet, the building is now stop No. 1 for first-time Madrid visitors. In fact, art historian Jonathan Brown has dubbed this spot, which is truly unmissable, “the most important museum in the world for European painting.” The collection of European art ranges from the 12th to the 20th centuries; the most famous works are “Las Meninas” by Diego Velazquez and “Third of May 1808” by Francisco Goya.
No entrance fee from Monday to Saturday from 6 pm to 8 pm, and on Sundays and holidays from 5 pm to 7 pm.

Reina Sofia Museum

One of Madrid’s heavy hitters, the Reina Sofia specializes in 20th-century Spanish art. While most Madrid visitors make a beeline for the Prado, this museum is often next on the agenda. The glass addition to the otherwise historic facade hints at the surprising gems within, including masterpieces focusing on feminism, dreams, the Spanish civil war, and most notably, Picasso’s "Guernica." Rotating exhibitions span the globe and touch upon thought-provoking, conversation-starting topics. This is one of the world’s largest museums dedicated to modern art, quite an impressive feat for a museum that opened in 1992. You'll find works from Pablo Picasso, Salvador Dalí, and other bold-faced names.
Free entry on Monday, Wednesday to Saturday from 7pm to 9pm, and Sunday from 1:30pm to 7pm.

Thyssen-Bornemisza Museum

The Museo Nacional Thyssen-Bornemisza, once an aristocratic mansion, falls somewhere between the avant-garde works at the Reina Sofia and the traditional Spanish art at the Prado. With an enormous collection of European art from the continent’s most respected masters, the museum is a must-see for anyone. The museum is known for its permanent collection of Italian Renaissance and French Impressionist masterpieces. Expect recognizable pieces from the likes of Dalí, El Greco, Monet, Picasso, and Rembrandt. Some works date back to the Medieval period; others are from the 20th century. There are even some 19th-century American paintings as well as interesting modern pop art.
You can see the collections of the great geniuses of world painting at this museum free of charge every Monday.

History Museums

Museums are the quickest way to absorb a city’s culture and history. They are a great way to surround yourself with the pieces of the past that you’ve always read about. It is also an opportunity to discover new things you never knew about.

Museum of History of Madrid

Discover Madrid's history in a different and enjoyable way thanks to the Museum of History of Madrid. Its permanent exhibition narrates the city's trajectory from its election as capital of the monarchy by Philip II to the first decades of the 20th century.

Museum of the Americas

The collection in the Museum of the Americas features various pieces from different cultures in the Americas. Through the unique items on display, visitors can take a journey through a broad period of history, ranging from prehistoric to modern times.

Railway Museum

The Railway Museum in Madrid is a vast, well-preserved building that tells the story of Spain's railroad system from its origins to the present day. You'll see real old locomotives up close and step into train cars that offer an interesting look at transportation in the 1800s.

National Museum of Anthropology

The National Anthropology Museum is a long-established museum that showcases the global cultures of Africa, Oceania, Asia, and Europe. The museum has a vast collection of items from all over the world, dating back to the 1800s.
